# Works From My Time at BNU

This repository is a selected archive of work from my time at Buckinghamshire New University.

I am placing it here as a kind of trail marker. Not a perfect monument, not a cleaned-up myth of the past, but a record of the strange, messy, living process of learning how to turn thought into systems, systems into games, and games into little worlds capable of holding questions bigger than the code running them.

BNU was where a lot of threads started to braid together for me: art, hospitality, games, story, simulation, philosophy, business, technical panic, technical delight, and the slow realisation that software is not only a tool. It is a place where intention becomes structure. A spell, in the practical sense. You write the rule, the machine obeys, and then suddenly you are responsible for the world you have made.

## Why This Archive Exists

I did not arrive at BNU through a perfectly straight academic road. My earlier education had been interrupted, and years of working in hospitality sat between the person who first wanted to make things and the person who finally returned to university to learn how.

That matters to this archive because the work did not begin from nothing. I brought years of management, pressure, people, stock, ordering, systems, mistakes, responsibility, and the constant practical problem of making complicated things function in the real world. At BNU, those experiences began turning into app ideas, game mechanics, design documents, simulated worlds, and code.

These are student projects, but they are also evidence of a restart: rebuilding technical confidence, learning to think through interaction, and discovering that ideas do not have to remain trapped inside essays or imagination. They can be given rules, movement, resistance, consequence, and form.

## What Is Here

This repository contains selected coursework and project documents from my BNU archive. It is mainly a document and development archive rather than a polished production codebase.

The selected work spans Foundation Year and Level 4 study across digital media, computing essentials, inquiry-based learning, game design, level design, programming concepts, maths for games, computer architecture, and the business of games.

The work gathered here moves through:

- digital media and the idea of games as immersive world-making
- an app concept shaped by my hospitality background, especially stock counting, ordering, point-of-sale data, and practical systems
- inquiry into computer graphics, virtual reality, simulation theory, and the strange border between a represented world and an experienced one
- game-design documentation and the attempt to understand games as more than products
- stealth level design through `Quantum Slip`
- Unity development, early programming struggle, and 2.5D arcade action through `Xscape Velocity`
- a business-of-games report exploring morality, empathy, restraint, responsibility, and the bigger questions interactive systems can carry

## The Journey Thread

Looking back, I can see the same question appearing in different disguises:

> What happens when an idea becomes interactive?

In digital media, that question looked like art becoming environment. In app development, it looked like hospitality experience becoming a practical tool. In computer graphics and virtual reality, it became a question about realities, simulations, perception, and what makes an experience feel present.

In `Quantum Slip`, it became stealth, silence, probability, uncertainty, and escape. In `Xscape Velocity`, it became movement, collision, pressure, failure, repair, and the simple joy of making something respond when I told it to.

Some of this work is rough. Some of it carries the marks of being early. That is the point.

This is not only an archive of finished assignments. It is an archive of becoming: the confusion before fluency, the moment Unity first felt like a maze, the moment a bug finally made sense, and the moment a game stopped being an essay about a game and became a thing with movement, gravity, and consequence.

## Where It Led

I later moved to the University of Sussex to continue developing the computing side of my work and build a broader foundation in computer science, digital media, and games.

That move does not replace or erase BNU. BNU is the first visible layer of the path I am still following.

Looking back now, I can see an early form of what I have since started calling **Philosophy of Interactive Systems**: the idea that games and interactive systems can operate as philosophical laboratories, places where abstract ideas become playable, choices become observable, and questions are encountered through action rather than only described in words.

I did not have that name for it yet. I only knew that I wanted games to do more than decorate an idea. I wanted the rules themselves to carry it.

## A Continuing Archive

This archive preserves the beginning of a journey rather than trying to turn it into a cleaner story than it was.

Some pieces show confidence. Others show the difficult stretch before confidence. Both matter, because progress becomes visible only when the earlier position has not been erased.

BNU was where ideas first began becoming systems, systems began becoming games, and games began becoming places where larger questions could live.

This is where some of the machinery began.
